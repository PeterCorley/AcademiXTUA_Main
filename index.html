Christian Ed: preparation for ministry, sacraments, loving service

Prophetic foundation (John the Baptist), Baptism as inauguration, Temptations and moral clarity, Anglican sacramental context, and service as mission.

John the Baptist: role, context, message

Prophetic role and urgency

  • Herald: Calls for repentance and readiness for God's reign; signals decisive divine action.
  • Eschatological tone: Urgency for ethical reorientation and concrete fruit.
  • Fruit of repentance: Generosity, honesty, fairness — practical signs of transformed life.

Historical context and core call

  • Isaiah's fulfillment: “Prepare the way of the Lord” situates John within prophetic expectation.
  • Core call: “Repent, for the kingdom of heaven has come near” — immediate and actionable.
  • Humility: Points to one “more powerful,” cultivating anticipation for Jesus' mission.

John's ministry disposes hearts toward receptive obedience, linking repentance to visible ethical fruit in community life.

Baptism of Jesus: identity, inauguration, solidarity

Event and meaning

  • Divine affirmation: Spirit descends; voice declares beloved sonship.
  • Public commencement: Sets Jesus' mission in motion with clarity of vocation.
  • Solidarity: Identifies with humanity; fulfills righteousness; anticipates sacrificial service.

Anglican sacramental frame

  • Dominical sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist instituted by Christ; ground identity and mission.
  • Seven sacraments: Baptism, Eucharist, Confirmation, Reconciliation, Marriage, Ordination, Anointing.
  • Visible signs: Outward rites mediating inward grace in communal practice.

Sacraments weave personal transformation into the Church's shared mission, shaping ethical identity and service.

Temptation narratives: mapping and emphasis

Matthew's emphasis

  • Obedience over appetite: Word precedes material need; mission is not reduced to consumption.
  • Reject spectacle: Do not test God for display; integrity over theatrics.
  • Allegiance to God: Refuse power via compromise; preserve vocation.

Luke's emphasis

  • Trust in provision: Dependence on God's timing and care.
  • No provocation: Faith without forcing proof.
  • Trajectory: Mission oriented toward Jerusalem and culmination.

Applications

  • Scripture and prayer: Anchor decisions; cultivate discernment.
  • Mission clarity: Reject materialism and spectacle; practice servant leadership.
  • Rule of life: Maintain rhythms that stabilize character and direction.

Core ministry and loving service

Five core activities

  • Preaching the Kingdom: nearness and nature of God's reign.
  • Teaching with authority: interpretation that transforms.
  • Healing and restoring: addressing suffering and exclusion.
  • Forgiving and reconciling: bridging humanity and God.
  • Confronting injustice: prophetic resistance to harm.

Third Mark of Mission

  • Principle: Respond to human need by loving service.
  • Practice: Pastoral visiting, mercy, volunteerism, outreach integrated into worship.
  • Formation: Service shapes communal identity and moral stamina.

PFPL: akademikong sulatin — kahulugan, estruktura, paghahanda

Sanaysay (lakbay, piktoryal, replektibo), buod, sintesis, bionote, katitikan, agenda, panukalang proyekto, at talumpati — long-form guidance and checklists.

Sanaysay: lakbay, piktoryal, replektibo

Lakbay-sanaysay

  • Layunin: Karanasan at pagninilay; magkuwento, magbigay-impormasyon, magbahagi ng aral.
  • Katangian: Personal na perspektiba; masinop sa kultura, tao, at lugar; konkretong eksena.
  • Estruktura: Hook → konteksto → karanasan (scene) → aral → tahimik na kongklusyon.

Pag-uugnay ng obserbasyon sa mas malawak na tema (katatagan, pakikipagkapwa) ay nagpapalalim ng aral at tumitibay ang kabuuan.

Piktoryal na sanaysay

  • Dominanteng larawan: Caption ~60 salita; cohesive visual story.
  • Pamantayan: Pokus, lohikal na estruktura, orihinalidad, kawilihan.
  • Layout: Teksto sa ilalim ng larawan; ritmo at motif para sa coherence.

Replektibong sanaysay

  • Unang panauhan: Tapat na tono; malinaw na aral at pananaw.
  • Bahagi: Introduksyon → katawan (paglilinaw) → kongklusyon (sintesis).
  • Sintesis: Pagsasama ng karanasan, teorya, at pananaw nang di nagiging abstrakto.

Buod, sintesis, bionote, katitikan, agenda, panukala

Buod

  • Neutral: Tapat sa kabuuang punto; sariling pananalita; iwas bias.
  • Hakbang: Tukuyin tema → alisin labis na detalye → panatilihin lohika.

Sintesis

  • Uri: Background, literature review, explanatory, thesis-driven.
  • Layunin: Pagsanib ng datos mula sa iba't ibang sanggunian; malinaw na ugnayan.

Bionote

  • Maikli at pormal: Pangalan, tungkulin, tagumpay, interes; tumpak ang gramatika.

Katitikan at agenda

  • Katitikan: Opisyal na tala ng desisyon, aksyon, dumalo, oras.
  • Agenda: Lohikal na listahan na may time allocations.

Panukalang proyekto

  • Nilalaman: Layunin, metodo, benepisyo, badyet, timeline; pamantayan ng tagumpay.
  • Gamit: Paghingi ng pahintulot/pondo; paglutas ng suliranin.

Panatilihin ang malinis na organisasyon: malinaw na layunin, ebidensya, at maayos na pagsipi ng sanggunian.

Talumpati: nilalaman, paraan, paghahanda

Nilalaman

  • Impormatibo: Paliwanag ng konsepto/isyu; maayos ang pag-unlad ng ideya.
  • Mapanghikayat: Ethos (kredibilidad), Logos (lohika), Pathos (emosyon) — balanse at tapat.
  • Explanatory: Stepwise na paliwanag; konkretong halimbawa.

Paraan at delivery

  • Impromptu: Mental map; panatilihin ang linaw.
  • Ekstemporanyo: Outline at rehearsed transitions; kontrol sa daloy.
  • Manuskripto: Buong teksto; bantayan ang monotony sa rehearsal.
  • Structure: Hook → roadmap → body (3–4 puntos) → kongklusyon.

Ethics: Tapat sa datos; iwas cherry-picking; maayos ang pagsipi at acknowledgement ng pinanggalingan.

Political science: foundations, power, governance, systems

Definitions and roots, governance principles, power sources and authority types, historical stages, and the Philippine political framework.

Foundations and governance principles

Definitions and roots

  • Politics: Art of governing well; leadership for the common good.
  • Political science: Study of the state, institutions, laws, processes, and power relations.
  • Roots: Polis (“city-state”); scire (“to know”).

Governance principles

  • Participation, rule of law, transparency, accountability, responsiveness.
  • Steering: Decision-making and implementation through power and authority.

Apply these principles to local issues; identify gaps, stakeholders, and remedies consistent with institutional mandates.

Power, authority, and structures

Sources and types of power

  • Force: Coercion to impose will.
  • Influence: Persuasion via ideas and relationships.
  • Authority: Institutionalized power recognized by subjects.
  • Common forms: Referent, coercive, legitimate, expert, informational.

Authority frameworks

  • Traditional: rooted in customs.
  • Rational-legal: rules-based, bureaucratic legitimacy.
  • Charismatic: exceptional personal appeal.

Consider distribution, circulation, and constraints across institutions; map actor relationships over time.

Historical stages and Philippine system

Stages of thought

  • Religious: Leadership and law seen as divine or divinely sanctioned.
  • Metaphysical: Institutions and laws humanly inspired.
  • Modern: Institutions improvable by rulers and subjects; dynamic adaptation.

Philippine political framework

  • GRP: Government of the Republic of the Philippines.
  • Head of State: President.
  • Form: Democracy, constitutional framework, separation of powers.

Emphasize rule of law, participation, and accountability as pillars of democratic practice and public trust.

Biology: animal circulation, regulation, and health

Diffusion limits, open vs closed systems, single vs double circulation, heart conduction and control, osmoregulation, and cardiovascular disease.

Limits of diffusion and need for circulation

Single-celled organisms

  • Short diffusion distances; high surface-area-to-volume ratio; passive diffusion meets metabolic needs.

Multicellular organisms

  • Long distances (e.g., lungs to peripheral cells) and lower SA:V require internal transport systems.
  • Circulatory systems deliver substrates and remove wastes efficiently under pressure.

Open vs closed circulatory systems

Open systems

  • Hemolymph bathes organs directly; sinuses allow less directed return.
  • Examples: arthropods, many mollusks.

Closed systems

  • Blood confined to vessels; higher pressure and efficiency; hemoglobin carries oxygen.
  • Examples: annelids, cephalopods, all vertebrates.
  • Supports higher activity and metabolic rates.

Circulatory pathways: single vs double loop

Single loop (fish)

  • Two-chambered heart: one atrium, one ventricle.
  • Heart → gills → body → heart; single pass per circuit.

Double loop (other vertebrates)

  • Pulmonary circuit: heart → lungs; gas exchange.
  • Systemic circuit: heart → body; delivery and return.
  • Better separation of oxygenated/deoxygenated blood; improved efficiency.

Human circulation: functions and pathway

Transport and homeostasis

  • Oxygen and nutrient delivery; waste removal; hormone distribution.
  • Immune defense (WBCs, antibodies), thermoregulation, fluid/pH balance.

Pathway and valves

  • Venae cavae → right atrium → right ventricle → pulmonary arteries → lungs → pulmonary veins → left atrium → left ventricle → aorta → systemic circulation.
  • AV valves with chordae tendineae prevent atrial backflow; semilunar valves prevent arterial backflow.

Heartbeat: conduction and control

Conduction

  • Myogenic rhythm; SAN initiates atrial contraction.
  • AVN → Bundle of His → Purkinje fibers; coordinate ventricular contraction.
  • Cycle phases: systole (contraction) and diastole (relaxation).

Rate control

  • Sympathetic noradrenaline increases SAN firing; parasympathetic acetylcholine reduces it.
  • Hormones: adrenaline (rate/force), thyroxine (baseline metabolic demand and HR).
  • Influences: pH, temperature, size, age, sex, activity, stress, health, medications, environment, smoking/alcohol.

Osmoregulation and excretion

Invertebrates

  • Simple tubular organs balance water/salt; remove metabolic wastes; structure adapts to habitat.

Vertebrates

  • Kidneys filter blood and produce urine; maintain salt-water balance; integrate with endocrine regulation.

Cardiovascular diseases

Arteriosclerosis and hypertension

  • Plaque narrows arteries and hardens walls; blood flow restricted.
  • Hypertension elevates arterial pressure; risks include stroke via vessel rupture.

Angina and myocardial infarction

  • Angina: chest pain from insufficient oxygen to heart muscle; warning sign.
  • Myocardial infarction: coronary occlusion; tissue death; severity depends on area affected.

UCSP: process of socialization

Context, content, mechanisms and stages, consequences, identity formation theories, norms, statuses, and roles.

Pillars and context

Context

  • Culture and history: shared norms, language, traditions.
  • Social structure: institutions and power dynamics.
  • Ascribed status: traits at birth; interpersonal relations and contact.

Content

  • Norms and rules: expectations of conduct.
  • Values and beliefs: standards of worth.
  • Roles: behavior tied to status.
  • Language and symbols: communication and meaning.
  • Skills: practical abilities for daily life.

Socialization transmits culture and equips individuals to participate in social life with predictable role performance.

Mechanisms, stages, and consequences

Mechanisms

  • Imitation and modeling; reward and punishment; instruction; anticipatory socialization.

Stages

  • Primary: early family learning.
  • Secondary: school, peers, workplace.
  • Resocialization: adopting new norms in new contexts.

Consequences

  • Individual: self and identity, impulse control, capacity for social life.
  • Society: transmission, role performance, order and stability, inequality reproduction.

Enculturation emphasizes culture learning, while socialization frames identity formation through norms and roles.

Identity theories and role dynamics

Identity formation

  • Freud: Id/Ego/Superego; unconscious dynamics; early childhood impact.
  • Piaget: cognitive development stages via active exploration.
  • Kohlberg: moral reasoning stages through dilemmas.
  • Erikson: eight psychosocial crises across lifespan.
  • Mead: social self via interaction; generalized other.

Norms, statuses, roles

  • Status: ascribed vs achieved positions.
  • Role: expected behavior tied to status.
  • Role conflict: competing expectations across statuses.
  • Role strain: tension within one status.
  • Role exit: leaving a role after doubt or change.

Balancing multiple statuses requires prioritization and negotiation to maintain coherent identity and performance.

Physics: forces, free body diagrams, First Law

Core forces and definitions, friction types, FBD techniques, and practical scenarios to apply the First Law of Motion.

Force types and definitions

Core forces

  • Applied: External push or pull.
  • Normal: Perpendicular contact force from a surface.
  • Gravity: Attraction due to mass; weight is gravitational force.
  • Tension: Pull through rope, string, or cable.
  • Air resistance: Drag opposing motion through air.

Friction and diagram basics

  • Friction: Static (no motion) vs kinetic (moving); both oppose relative motion.
  • FBDs: Isolate object; arrows for forces; label type, direction, magnitude.
  • Components: Resolve parallel and perpendicular on inclines; find net force for equilibrium or acceleration.

Newton's First Law (inertia) states that an object maintains its state of motion unless acted on by a net external force.

Practice scenarios

  • Box on table: Gravity down; normal up; applied force right; friction left; negligible air resistance indoors.
  • Inclined plane: Resolve gravity into parallel and perpendicular components; include friction if present.
  • Suspended mass: Tension balances weight at rest; for multiple cords, use vector resolution and angles.

Chemistry: solutions, colligative properties, concentrations

Solubility and miscibility factors, electrolytes vs non-electrolytes, vapor pressure relations, and common concentration expressions.

Solubility, miscibility, colligative properties

Solubility drivers

  • Polarity (“like dissolves like”); miscibility for liquids.
  • Particle size and stirring affect dissolution rate.
  • Temperature often increases solubility of solids; decreases gas solubility.
  • Pressure strongly affects gases (e.g., Henry's relation).

Colligative properties

  • Depend on particle number: boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, vapor pressure lowering, osmotic pressure.
  • Electrolytes ionize and conduct; non-electrolytes do not.

Solutions exhibit predictable changes based on solute quantity, not identity; this underpins many practical applications.

Vapor pressure and Raoult's relation

  • Non-volatile solute lowers solvent's vapor pressure.
  • Relation (ideal): P(solution solvent) = X(solvent) × P°(pure solvent).

Concentration expressions and practice

Percent and fraction

  • % m/m = (mass solute / mass solution) × 100
  • % v/v = (volume solute / volume solution) × 100
  • % m/v = (mass solute / volume solution) × 100
  • Mole fraction: xA = nA / (nA + nB), xA + xB = 1

Molality, molarity, ppm

  • Molality m = moles solute / kilogram solvent
  • Molarity M = moles solute / liter solution
  • PPM = (mass solute / mass solution) × 10^6

Practice: compute moles from mass and molar mass; then calculate M, m, and x using provided totals.

Mandarin: everyday language

Family and hobbies vocabulary, food and drinks, sentence frames, tones, and a steady practice routine.

Family members and core Q&A

  • 你好！你家有几口人？ Ni hao! Ni jia you jǐ kou rén? — Hello! How many people are in your family?
  • 我家有五口人。 Wo jia you wu kou ren. — There are five people in my family.
  • 他们是谁？ Tamen shi shei? — Who are they?
  • 这是我爸爸、妈妈、哥哥、妹妹和我。 Zhè shi wo baba, mama, gēge, mèimei hé wo. — Father, mother, older brother, younger sister, and me.

Pronunciation: practice tone pairs daily; distinguish retroflex zh/ch/sh/r from palatal j/q/x; mind finals (-ang vs -an, -eng vs -en).

Vocabulary

  • 看电影 kàn diànyǐng — watching movies
  • 跳舞 tiàowǔ — dancing
  • 唱歌 chànggē — singing
  • 画画 huàhuà — drawing
  • 打篮球 dǎ lánqiú — basketball
  • 打羽毛球 dǎ yǔmáoqiú — badminton
  • 游泳 yóuyǒng — swimming
  • 打游戏 dǎ yóuxì — gaming

Sentence frames

  • 我喜欢＿＿。 Wǒ xǐhuan __. — I like __.

Practice: ask preferences using “你喜欢…吗？” and respond with 喜欢 / 不喜欢; expand with time and place.

THS intramurals 2025

Eligibility, team line-ups, conduct, protests, tournament format, ranking, and event-specific rules.

Eligibility, uniforms, banners

  • Bonafide Trinity High School students only; minimum 1 and maximum 2 events per student (MAPEH requirement); parade/cheer not counted.
  • Submit 1×1 photo for galleries (basketball, volleyball, dodgeball); parent approval by deadline.
  • Uniforms: team shirts with numbers (front/back, 1–99); sports goggles allowed; reading glasses prohibited for ball games.
  • Prepare team banner (tarpaulin) by the specified deadline for opening day.

Line-ups, encoding, conduct

Entries and submissions

  • Encode via official Google Sheets; adviser + up to two student coordinators.
  • Include gallery photos for ball games; no changes after submission; deadlines strictly enforced.

Behavior and compliance

  • No intoxicants; respect officials; clarifications via game captain.
  • Violence or walkouts lead to disqualification; avoid stalling and influencing officials.

Protests, defaults, incomplete teams

Protests

  • Mark “Game under protest” on score sheet; submit written protest within 30 minutes post-game.
  • Only technical matters; judgment calls are not contestable.

Defaults and participation

  • 15-minute defaulting time across events; minimum player counts enforced per event.

Tournament format and ranking highlights

Formats

  • Single round robin for basketball, dodgeball, esports, volleyball; scheduling via draw lots.

Ranking and ties

  • Points systems per event; ties broken via bench cheering totals (≥3 teams) or circuit game (2 teams).

Event mechanics highlights

  • Basketball (boys): Two 8-minute halves (running); last 2 minutes of 2nd half stop time; timeout and tech foul rules; overtime format.
  • Basketball 3×3 (girls): 10 minutes or 15 points; 10-second shot clock; roster minimums.
  • Volleyball: Elims/semis single set to 30; finals best-of-three to 15 (max 17); no libero.
  • Dodgeball: Coed lineup; live ball rules; boundaries; opening rush and 5-second violation; honor system with monitor.
  • Esports (ML, COD BR): Device rules, drafting, tie-breakers, anti-cheating, disqualification grounds; ping check and compliance photos.
  • Parade, mural, bench cheering: Criteria, time allocations, penalties, judging structure.
  • Games of the General: Movement rules; piece interactions; win conditions.
  • Quiz Bee: Rounds (easy/average/difficult), lifelines, scoring, tiebreakers, empowerment focus.

Awards

  • General Championship trophies/tokens for top teams; event banners/tokens; special awards (Mythical, MVP).
